Ghibli is named after the Libyan word "ghibli," which refers to the hot desert wind in the Sahara. The name reflects the studio's aspiration to "bring a new wind to the Japanese animation industry." True to its name, Studio Ghibli has followed its own path, portraying the human spirit and the essence of life with honesty, without being bound by commercialism or industry conventions. The studio’s approach — prioritizing the message they want to share over chasing trends — remains the source of its unique charm to this day.

1. A Day at Ghibli: Moments of Everyday Life

The process of completing an animation is a quieter, more subtle accumulation of everyday moments than you might expect. Behind the fantastic worlds, vivid characters, and touching scenes we see in theaters lie countless small, quiet days. This is how a day at Studio Ghibli begins. In the morning, each staff member starts their day in their own way. Some ride bicycles, others quietly brew a cup of coffee and sit at their desks. Original animators at their monitors pick up pencils to draw the first lines on paper, while background sketchers transfer their work onto digital screens beside them. It’s like intricately meshed gears turning steadily, not fast but never stopping. People at Ghibli treat each other not as 'team members' but more like 'neighbors.' They naturally share feedback as they pass in the hallways, and when stuck, they don’t hesitate to ask for advice. Inspiration sometimes comes from conversations over coffee or a doodle on the corner of a desk that sparks an idea for a scene. A single word or a smile becomes a piece of the work. At lunchtime, small groups gather in the cafeteria to chat about simple things: walks taken over the weekend, movies watched last night, or the quirks of their cats at home. In these ordinary conversations, the emotion of the work quietly takes root. Most stories at Ghibli begin in such seemingly unremarkable moments. A day at Ghibli is not just time spent making animation. It is time when people meet, and stories and emotions permeate. These seemingly ordinary days eventually come together to create wonderful works. Magic, if you look closely, grows within these ordinary days. The reason Studio Ghibli’s animations always feel warm and alive is right here. It’s because the ‘truth of everyday life’ beyond fantasy always flows through the screen.

2. A Frame Begins at the Artisan’s Fingertips: The Original Drawing Process

Studio Ghibli’s animations always begin with a single pencil. Not flashy graphics or intricate 3D rendering, but a single sketch. A carefully drawn line on paper becomes a living being and a story. And at the starting point is the original animator’s hand. The atmosphere in the drawing room is exceptionally quiet. Without screen, music, or dialogue, only the sound of pencils sliding across paper fills the space. Animators repeat the same motions dozens or even hundreds of times to bring a character’s movement to life. A fleeting glance, hair swaying in the wind, a flicker of emotion in the eyes—everything must flow naturally within one scene. The subtle changes in pressure at the fingertips, even the barely visible differences in line thickness affect the character’s vitality. Therefore, animators stay focused, stop, and redraw repeatedly. If the line doesn’t satisfy them, they erase it without hesitation and start over. The completed original drawing is not just a rough sketch but a ‘trace of thought’ filled with countless worries and decisions. The reason Ghibli’s characters feel so alive is here. The artisan’s eyes do not stop at drawing shapes but read emotions and intentions beyond. Their hands transfer feelings, create rhythm, and breathe life into movement. The camera often zooms in on their fingertips. The hands immersed in work until the eraser dust scatters or the pencil lead wears down. More stories are hidden there than in the finished scene. A single drawing is for a brief moment, but it holds hours and days of concentration, persistence, and a deep passion to bring something ‘alive.’ Ghibli’s magic starts right at those fingertips. The care put into even the tiniest unnoticed line ultimately creates a scene that moves the audience’s hearts.

3. Emotions Completed with Light and Color: The Art of Background Painting

When we think of Ghibli’s world, what first comes to mind may not be the characters but the ‘scenes.’ A rural road dyed in the evening glow, fields swaying in the wind, a bus stop sleeping under tree shade. These scenes are not mere backgrounds but another protagonist holding stories. Ghibli’s background paintings approach artistic completeness. The brush of the background artist does more than depict scenery. The sky’s colors vary with time and emotion, and the light and shadow on leaves reflect the characters’ inner feelings. Even a single blade of grass holds traces of the wind passing by, and rain-soaked dirt roads leave a lingering impression. Though the painting is still, viewers feel movement. This comes from the temperature of color. An alley soaked in blue dawn light, a valley bathed in warm evening sun, a misty forest path—each background gives a sense of flowing time. Background artists layer colors dozens of times and carefully adjust the direction of light to capture invisible air currents. Sometimes the camera lingers on empty scenes without characters. Time flows with no sound or movement, yet the audience feels emotions. Quiet loneliness, warm relief, or an inexplicable longing—these are emotions conveyed through the brush tip. Ghibli’s backgrounds are not just stages explaining space but speak emotions and complete stories. The light, color, and careful handwork in the paintings become another line of dialogue and memorable scenes that move the audience’s hearts.

4. Another World Created by Sound: The Recording Studio and Music Work

If the world we see is Ghibli’s landscape, the world we hear is completed by music and sound. In a quiet recording studio separated from the outside, another kind of magic is underway. This is the space where sound turns into emotion, opening the door to an invisible world. Joe Hisaishi’s music is the identity that makes Ghibli’s works distinctly Ghibli. His melodies are simple yet deep, not flashy but linger in the heart. Before the first note, countless scripts, drawings, and storyboards are spread out in his studio. He carefully contemplates the atmosphere of scenes, the emotions of characters, and the meaning of unspoken spaces to create the music. Sometimes he sits at the piano for hours, repeating a single phrase to adjust the emotion. Inside the recording booth, another scene unfolds. Voice actors don’t simply read lines—they become the characters. Breathing, crying, laughing, and blending into the scene. Though separated by walls, the director and sound staff guide every nuance. Sounds of footsteps on gravel, wind rustling in trees, raindrops falling softly, and distant chatter. Each sound is recorded meticulously and layered with care. Together with the music, they form a soundscape that perfectly harmonizes with the visuals. The final mix is the moment when the whole world becomes alive. The invisible voices and sounds bring a deep sense of presence, making the story’s reality tangible. Ghibli’s magic is not only seen but also felt through sound.

5. The Spirit of Collaboration: The Production Team’s Heart

Studio Ghibli’s works are the product of countless creators’ hearts and sweat. From original animators and background artists to sound staff, editors, producers, and directors, many people collaborate seamlessly. The heart of this collaboration is communication and trust. Even when opinions clash or difficulties arise, they share the same goal—to create something meaningful. Daily meetings, informal chats, and sometimes heated discussions build mutual understanding. In the studio, a lively atmosphere coexists with serious focus. Laughter and encouragement echo through the corridors, as well as quiet concentration. This collective spirit turns individual talents into an inseparable whole. Each person’s passion and responsibility resonate, creating a creative atmosphere where great work can be born. Ghibli’s animation is more than just a product—it is a shared dream made real by many hands.

6. The Final Step: Editing and Completion

After all drawings, backgrounds, sounds, and music come together, the final step is editing. In this stage, every frame is reviewed repeatedly, scenes are refined, and timing is adjusted. The editor works closely with the director to ensure that the story flows smoothly and emotions hit at the right moment. Sometimes a scene is shortened or lengthened; sometimes music cues change; sometimes a subtle gesture is added or removed. The goal is a seamless blend that brings the audience fully into the story’s world. When the final cut is completed, the animation is ready for the screen. But the process never truly ends; each viewing brings new discoveries and emotions. Studio Ghibli’s works continue to live and breathe long after the final frame is shown, touching hearts across generations.
